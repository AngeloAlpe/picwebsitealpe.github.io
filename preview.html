Who are the Hanunoo Mangyan?

The Hanunoo Mangyan are one of the eight Mangyan groups indigenous to Mindoro, Philippines. Known for preserving their ancestral traditions, they continue to practice ancient writing, bamboo poetry, weaving, music, and oral storytelling traditions even in the modern era.

Their communities are often located in mountainous and forested areas, where culture and nature remain deeply intertwined like roots beneath rainforest soil.

Surat Mangyan

One of the most extraordinary treasures of the Hanunoo Mangyan is their ancient writing system called Surat Mangyan.

Unlike ordinary alphabets, this script is syllabic and traditionally carved into bamboo using knives. It belongs to the family of ancient Philippine scripts related to Baybayin.

In 1999, the script became part of UNESCO's Memory of the World Register, preserving its importance as one of the Philippines’ living pre-colonial writing systems.

“A language carved on bamboo becomes memory that cannot easily disappear.”

Ambahan Poetry

Ambahan is the poetic heartbeat of the Hanunoo Mangyan people. These poems are traditionally carved into bamboo and recited through rhythmic chanting. Most Ambahan poems follow a seven-syllable structure and contain messages about love, friendship, advice, journeys, and reflections about life.

Arts, Weaving & Music

Traditional Weaving

Hanunoo textiles use geometric embroidery, indigenous dyes, and nature-inspired patterns reflecting harmony with the environment.

Bamboo Craftsmanship

Bamboo becomes both canvas and instrument, transformed into poetry carvings, containers, tools, and artistic expressions.

Music & Courtship

Courtship often includes flutes, guitars, violins, and poetic serenades, creating traditions where music and language intertwine beautifully.

Ginaw Bilog

Ginaw Bilog became one of the most important cultural guardians of the Hanunoo Mangyan. Through preserving Ambahan poetry and the Mangyan script, he became the first recipient of the prestigious GAMABA or National Living Treasure Award.

He once described Ambahan as:

“The key to the Mangyan soul.”
